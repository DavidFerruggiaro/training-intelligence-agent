# "Living High-Training Low": Effect of Moderate-Altitude Acclimatization with Low-Altitude Training on Performance

## Metadata
- **Authors**: Benjamin D. Levine, James Stray-Gundersen
- **Year**: 1997
- **Journal**: Journal of Applied Physiology
- **DOI**: 10.1152/jappl.1997.83.1.102

## Abstract

Altitude training has long been employed by endurance athletes seeking to enhance performance through stimulation of erythropoiesis, yet traditional live high-train high approaches often compromise training quality due to reduced oxygen availability during intense sessions. This landmark study investigated a novel paradigm of living at moderate altitude to obtain the acclimatization benefits while training at low altitude to maintain training intensity and velocity. Thirty-nine competitive distance runners were randomly assigned to one of three groups for a four-week intervention: live high-train low (living at two thousand five hundred meters, training at one thousand two hundred fifty meters), live high-train high (both living and training at two thousand five hundred meters), or live low-train low (living and training at one hundred fifty meters as a sea-level control). All groups followed identical training programs prescribed relative to individual lactate threshold. The live high-train low group demonstrated significant increases in red cell mass volume (average increase of five percent), erythropoietin concentration (initial surge of two hundred percent above baseline within twenty-four hours of altitude exposure), maximal oxygen uptake (average increase of three percent), and five-thousand meter time trial performance (average improvement of thirteen point four seconds). The live high-train high group showed similar hematological adaptations including increased red cell mass and erythropoietin response but did not demonstrate equivalent performance improvements, likely due to a reduction in training velocity and accumulated fatigue from chronic hypoxic training. The live low-train low control group showed no significant changes in any hematological or performance measure. The critical finding was that the live high-train low paradigm uniquely combined the erythropoietic benefits of altitude acclimatization with the maintenance of training quality at lower elevation, producing a net performance benefit not achieved by either traditional altitude training or sea-level training alone. The erythropoietic response was initiated within the first twenty-four to forty-eight hours of altitude exposure and continued throughout the four-week exposure period. Substantial individual variation in response was observed, with some athletes demonstrating robust erythropoietic and performance responses while others showed minimal adaptation despite identical exposure protocols.

## Key Findings

- The live high-train low paradigm produced a five percent increase in red cell mass volume and a three percent increase in maximal oxygen uptake over four weeks
- Five-thousand meter time trial performance improved by an average of thirteen point four seconds in the live high-train low group only
- Traditional live high-train high altitude training stimulated equivalent erythropoietic responses but did not produce equivalent performance gains due to compromised training quality
- Erythropoietin concentrations surged within twenty-four to forty-eight hours of altitude exposure and remained elevated throughout the acclimatization period
- Substantial individual variation existed in the erythropoietic and performance response to altitude exposure, suggesting genetic and physiological mediators of the altitude training effect

## Practical Implications

Endurance athletes seeking performance benefits from altitude exposure should adopt the live high-train low model, residing at moderate altitude between two thousand and two thousand five hundred meters while descending to lower elevations for key training sessions. A minimum exposure of four weeks appears necessary to realize meaningful erythropoietic and performance adaptations. Athletes and coaches should be aware that individual responses to altitude training vary considerably, and some athletes may be classified as non-responders based on their erythropoietic capacity. Simulated altitude environments using nitrogen dilution tents or hypoxic generators offer practical alternatives for athletes unable to relocate to natural altitude locations. Key high-intensity training sessions should always be performed at low altitude or in normoxic conditions to preserve training velocity, neuromuscular recruitment, and metabolic stimulus quality. Monitoring of reticulocyte counts and hemoglobin mass can help identify individual responsiveness to altitude exposure within the first two weeks.
