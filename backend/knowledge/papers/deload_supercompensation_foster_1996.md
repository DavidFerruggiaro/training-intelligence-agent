# Monitoring Training in Athletes With Reference to Overtraining Syndrome

## Metadata
- **Authors**: Carl Foster
- **Year**: 1998
- **Journal**: Medicine and Science in Sports and Exercise
- **DOI**: 10.1097/00005768-199807000-00023

## Abstract

Effective training monitoring is essential for balancing the competing demands of progressive overload and adequate recovery, yet practical monitoring systems that can be readily implemented in diverse athletic settings remain underdeveloped. This paper presents a comprehensive framework for training load quantification using the session rating of perceived exertion (sRPE) method and explores its application for identifying optimal recovery and deload timing to prevent overtraining and facilitate supercompensation. The sRPE method involves athletes rating the global intensity of each training session on a modified zero-to-ten category ratio scale, which is then multiplied by session duration in minutes to derive an arbitrary unit of internal training load. Longitudinal monitoring of sRPE-derived training loads enables calculation of training monotony (the ratio of daily mean load to its standard deviation) and training strain (the product of weekly load and monotony), both of which have been associated with illness incidence and maladaptive training outcomes. High monotony values, reflecting uniform daily training loads without adequate variation, are associated with elevated risk of illness and overreaching, even when absolute training loads are not excessive. This finding provides physiological justification for incorporating planned variation including deload periods within training microcycles and mesocycles. Analysis of training data from competitive athletes across multiple sports demonstrates that periods of deliberately reduced training load following intensive training blocks are associated with subsequent performance improvements consistent with the supercompensation model, provided the recovery period is of sufficient duration to allow physiological restoration. The sRPE method provides an accessible and validated approach for monitoring training load that requires no specialized equipment and can be implemented across all sports and competitive levels, making it a practical foundation for individualized training load management and evidence-based deload timing.

## Key Findings

- The session RPE method (intensity rating multiplied by duration) provides a valid and practical measure of internal training load across all sports
- Training monotony (low day-to-day variation in load) is independently associated with illness risk and maladaptive outcomes, justifying planned load variation
- Training strain (weekly load multiplied by monotony) predicts illness incidence more effectively than absolute training load alone
- Deliberately reduced training loads following intensive blocks facilitate supercompensation when recovery duration is individually appropriate
- The sRPE method requires no specialized equipment and achieves correlations of 0.75-0.90 with heart rate-based training load measures

## Practical Implications

All athletes and coaches should implement systematic training load monitoring using the session RPE method as a minimum standard practice. After each training session, athletes should provide an overall intensity rating on a zero-to-ten scale, which is multiplied by session duration to calculate session load. Weekly calculations of training monotony and strain can identify periods of excessive uniformity or accumulated fatigue that warrant training modification. Deload periods should be strategically timed when monotony or strain values exceed established thresholds, or when weekly load progression has been sustained for three to four consecutive weeks. The simplicity and zero cost of the sRPE method make it immediately implementable in any training environment, from professional sport to recreational fitness programs.
