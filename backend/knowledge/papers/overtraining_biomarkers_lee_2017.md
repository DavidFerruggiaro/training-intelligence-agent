# Biomarkers in Sports and Exercise: Tracking Health, Performance, and Recovery in Athletes

## Metadata
- **Authors**: Elaine C. Lee, Melissa S. Fragala, Sjoerd M. Kavouras, Robin M. Queen, Jeffrey L. Pryor, Douglas J. Casa
- **Year**: 2017
- **Journal**: Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research
- **DOI**: 10.1519/JSC.0000000000001831

## Abstract

The identification of reliable biomarkers for monitoring training status, health, and recovery represents a central challenge in sports science. This comprehensive review evaluates the current evidence for blood-based, salivary, and urinary biomarkers used to assess overtraining risk, recovery status, and readiness to train in competitive athletes. The review categorizes biomarkers into functional domains including markers of muscle damage (creatine kinase, lactate dehydrogenase, myoglobin), inflammatory status (C-reactive protein, interleukin-6, tumor necrosis factor-alpha), hormonal balance (cortisol, testosterone, growth hormone, insulin-like growth factor-1), immune function (immunoglobulin A, leukocyte counts, natural killer cell activity), oxidative stress (malondialdehyde, glutathione peroxidase, superoxide dismutase), and metabolic status (blood lactate, ammonia, iron parameters). Creatine kinase remains the most widely used marker of muscle damage but demonstrates high inter-individual variability and delayed peak responses, limiting its utility for acute training decisions. The free testosterone-to-cortisol ratio has shown promise as an indicator of anabolic-catabolic balance, with sustained reductions below baseline potentially indicating non-functional overreaching. Salivary immunoglobulin A has emerged as a practical non-invasive marker of mucosal immunity, with declining trends associated with increased upper respiratory tract infection risk during intensive training periods. Salivary cortisol offers a non-invasive alternative to blood sampling for monitoring stress responses, though standardized collection protocols are essential for reliable interpretation. The review emphasizes that no single biomarker provides sufficient diagnostic power in isolation, and that multivariate monitoring approaches combining biological markers with performance data, subjective wellness indicators, and training load metrics offer the most comprehensive assessment of athlete readiness and recovery status. Practical considerations including cost, invasiveness, time-to-result, and measurement reliability are discussed to guide practitioners in selecting appropriate biomarker panels for their specific athletic populations and monitoring objectives.

## Key Findings

- No single biomarker is sufficient for diagnosing overtraining; multivariate approaches combining biological markers with performance and subjective data are recommended
- The free testosterone-to-cortisol ratio provides useful information about anabolic-catabolic balance when tracked longitudinally against individual baselines
- Salivary immunoglobulin A shows promise as a non-invasive predictor of upper respiratory infection risk during heavy training periods
- Creatine kinase, while widely used for muscle damage assessment, has high inter-individual variability that limits cross-athlete comparisons
- Salivary cortisol provides a practical non-invasive stress monitoring option when standardized collection protocols are followed

## Practical Implications

Sports science practitioners should develop multivariate athlete monitoring systems that combine selected biomarkers with training load data, performance testing, and subjective wellness questionnaires rather than relying on any single metric. Salivary markers (cortisol, immunoglobulin A) offer practical non-invasive options suitable for frequent monitoring in team sport settings. Individual baseline values and normal ranges should be established for each athlete during pre-season periods to enable meaningful longitudinal tracking. The testosterone-to-cortisol ratio should be monitored across mesocycles to detect sustained shifts in anabolic-catabolic balance that may precede performance decrements. Cost-effective monitoring protocols should be tailored to the specific needs and resources of each athletic program.
