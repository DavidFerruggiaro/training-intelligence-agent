# Prevention, Diagnosis, and Treatment of the Overtraining Syndrome: Joint Consensus Statement of the European College of Sport Science and the American College of Sports Medicine

## Metadata
- **Authors**: Romain Meeusen, Martine Duclos, Carl Foster, Andrew Fry, Michael Gleeson, David Nieman, John Raglin, Gerard Rietjens, Juergen Steinacker, Axel Urhausen
- **Year**: 2013
- **Journal**: Medicine and Science in Sports and Exercise
- **DOI**: 10.1249/MSS.0b013e318279a10a

## Abstract

Overtraining syndrome (OTS) represents one of the most significant threats to athlete health and performance, yet its prevention, diagnosis, and management remain challenging due to the absence of definitive diagnostic biomarkers and the overlap of symptoms with other clinical conditions. This joint consensus statement from the European College of Sport Science and the American College of Sports Medicine provides a comprehensive framework for understanding the continuum from functional overreaching through non-functional overreaching to overtraining syndrome, and offers evidence-based recommendations for prevention, diagnosis, and treatment. The distinction between functional overreaching (FOR) and non-functional overreaching (NFOR) centers on the time course of recovery: FOR involves short-term performance decrements lasting days to weeks that are followed by supercompensation, while NFOR results in performance stagnation or decline lasting weeks to months. OTS represents the most severe end of the spectrum, with prolonged performance decrements persisting for months or longer, accompanied by psychological and neuroendocrine disturbances. The consensus panel emphasizes that OTS is a diagnosis of exclusion, requiring systematic evaluation to rule out organic disease, nutritional deficiencies, and psychological disorders. Key warning signs include persistent unexplained performance decline despite adequate recovery, altered resting heart rate and heart rate variability patterns, disturbed sleep despite fatigue, increased perception of effort during standard training loads, mood disturbances including increased irritability and depression, and recurrent illness. Hormonal markers including the testosterone-to-cortisol ratio and overnight urinary catecholamine excretion may provide supportive evidence but lack sufficient sensitivity and specificity for definitive diagnosis. The most reliable diagnostic approach combines performance testing with maximal exercise challenge protocols, monitoring both the acute hormonal response and subsequent recovery kinetics. Prevention strategies emphasize periodized training with adequate recovery periods, individualized training load monitoring, and attention to psychosocial stressors that may compound the effects of physical training stress.

## Key Findings

- Overtraining exists on a continuum from functional overreaching (days of recovery) to non-functional overreaching (weeks to months) to overtraining syndrome (months or longer)
- OTS remains a diagnosis of exclusion with no single definitive biomarker; a combination of performance testing, hormonal profiling, and clinical assessment is required
- The testosterone-to-cortisol ratio and catecholamine excretion provide supportive but not definitive diagnostic evidence for OTS
- Persistent unexplained performance decline, disturbed sleep, mood changes, and elevated perceived exertion during standard training are key warning indicators
- Prevention through periodized training, individualized load monitoring, and psychosocial stress management is far more effective than attempting to treat established OTS

## Practical Implications

Coaches and sport science practitioners should implement systematic monitoring programs that track training load, performance responses, sleep quality, mood states, and subjective well-being to detect early warning signs of non-functional overreaching before progression to overtraining syndrome. The inclusion of regular standardized performance tests allows objective detection of unexplained performance decrements. Training programs must incorporate planned recovery periods and deload phases, and coaches should recognize that psychosocial stressors such as academic pressure, travel, and personal challenges contribute to overall allostatic load and may reduce an athlete's capacity to tolerate physical training stress. When OTS is suspected, the primary treatment is prolonged rest and a gradual return to training under medical supervision, making prevention the far superior strategy.
