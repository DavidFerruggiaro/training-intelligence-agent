# Is Blood Lactate a Meaningful Marker of Subclinical Mitochondrial Dysfunction and Insulin Resistance?

## Metadata
- **Authors**: Inigo San-Millan, George A. Brooks
- **Year**: 2018
- **Journal**: Medicine and Science in Sports and Exercise
- **DOI**: 10.1249/MSS.0000000000001437

## Abstract

Mitochondrial function represents a central determinant of both metabolic health and endurance exercise capacity, yet practical assessments of mitochondrial status remain limited in clinical and sports science settings. This paper proposes blood lactate concentration during submaximal exercise as a meaningful and accessible marker of mitochondrial function, with direct implications for training prescription in the low-intensity zone commonly referred to as zone two. The crossover concept of substrate utilization during exercise demonstrates that the transition from predominantly fat oxidation to increasing carbohydrate reliance is governed by mitochondrial oxidative capacity. Individuals with higher mitochondrial density and enzymatic activity can sustain greater absolute and relative exercise intensities while maintaining fat as the primary fuel substrate, reflected in lower blood lactate concentrations at matched submaximal workloads. Zone two training, defined as exercise at intensities corresponding to the maximal rate of fat oxidation and lactate concentrations of approximately one point five to two point zero millimoles per liter, provides the primary stimulus for enhancing mitochondrial biogenesis, mitochondrial density, and the expression of oxidative enzymes including citrate synthase and cytochrome c oxidase. Longitudinal training studies demonstrate that sustained zone two training programs of twelve to sixteen weeks duration produce significant increases in fat oxidation capacity, reductions in submaximal blood lactate, and improvements in lactate clearance kinetics. Importantly, the relationship between blood lactate kinetics and mitochondrial function appears relevant beyond athletic performance: emerging evidence links impaired mitochondrial oxidative capacity with elevated resting and submaximal lactate concentrations in populations with insulin resistance, type two diabetes, and metabolic syndrome. This framework positions zone two training intensity as a therapeutic exercise prescription for metabolic health improvement and suggests that blood lactate profiling during graded exercise testing provides a practical window into mitochondrial function for both athletic and clinical populations.

## Key Findings

- Blood lactate concentration during submaximal exercise serves as a practical proxy marker of mitochondrial oxidative capacity in both athletes and clinical populations
- Zone two training at intensities corresponding to one point five to two millimoles per liter blood lactate provides the optimal stimulus for mitochondrial biogenesis
- Twelve to sixteen weeks of consistent zone two training significantly increases fat oxidation capacity and reduces submaximal blood lactate concentrations
- Impaired mitochondrial function reflected in elevated submaximal lactate is associated with insulin resistance and metabolic syndrome in non-athletic populations
- The lactate threshold framework provides a unifying model connecting exercise physiology with metabolic health assessment

## Practical Implications

Athletes and coaches should incorporate substantial zone two training volume at intensities corresponding to blood lactate concentrations of approximately one point five to two millimoles per liter. Graded exercise testing with blood lactate measurement provides the most precise method for identifying this intensity zone individually. Where lactate testing is unavailable, zone two intensity can be approximated as the highest intensity at which an athlete can sustain a normal conversation without breathlessness. This training intensity should comprise the foundation of endurance programs, as it provides the primary stimulus for the mitochondrial adaptations that underpin both athletic performance and metabolic health. Clinicians working with metabolically compromised populations should consider zone two exercise prescription as a targeted intervention for improving mitochondrial function.
