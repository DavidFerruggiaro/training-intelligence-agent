# Polarized Training Has Greater Impact on Key Endurance Variables Than Threshold, High Intensity, or High Volume Training

## Metadata
- **Authors**: Thomas Stoggl, Billy Sperlich
- **Year**: 2014
- **Journal**: Frontiers in Physiology
- **DOI**: 10.3389/fphys.2014.00033

## Abstract

While observational evidence suggests elite endurance athletes favor a polarized training intensity distribution, controlled experimental comparisons between training models remain scarce. This nine-week randomized controlled trial directly compared four distinct training approaches in well-trained endurance athletes to determine which model produced the greatest physiological and performance adaptations. Forty-eight competitive endurance athletes (runners and cyclists) with a minimum of five years training background were randomly assigned to one of four groups: high-volume training (HVT), threshold training (THR), high-intensity interval training (HIIT), or polarized training (POL). All groups trained for the same total duration, with the POL group performing approximately seventy-seven percent of sessions at low intensity and the remainder as high-intensity intervals, the THR group performing the majority of sessions near the lactate threshold, the HIIT group completing a high proportion of interval sessions, and the HVT group performing primarily low-intensity continuous work. Pre- and post-testing included incremental exercise tests to exhaustion, time-to-exhaustion trials, body composition assessment, and blood lactate profiling. The polarized training group demonstrated the largest improvements across nearly all measured variables, including peak oxygen uptake, time to exhaustion, peak power output, and velocity at the four-millimole lactate threshold. Notably, the polarized group improved VO2peak by an average of six percent, compared to approximately one to three percent in the other groups. The threshold training group showed the smallest overall improvements despite reporting the highest perceived training monotony. High-intensity interval training produced moderate gains but was associated with higher ratings of perceived fatigue and greater dropout rates. These results provide the first direct experimental evidence that a polarized training approach produces superior multivariable endurance adaptations compared to threshold, high-intensity, or high-volume models in already well-trained athletes.

## Key Findings

- Polarized training produced the largest improvements in VO2peak (+6.0%) compared to threshold (+1.2%), HIIT (+2.8%), and high-volume (+1.8%) groups
- Time-to-exhaustion improved most in the polarized group (+17.4%) versus threshold (+8.8%), HIIT (+10.1%), and high-volume (+9.2%) groups
- Threshold training yielded the smallest overall improvements despite the highest perceived training monotony scores
- The HIIT-only group experienced higher dropout rates and fatigue ratings, suggesting sustainability concerns with this approach
- Polarized training improved performance across the widest range of physiological variables simultaneously

## Practical Implications

This study provides strong experimental support for adopting a polarized training model over threshold-focused or exclusively high-intensity approaches in well-trained endurance athletes. Coaches should design mesocycles where approximately three-quarters of training sessions are performed at genuinely low intensity with the remaining sessions featuring high-intensity intervals well above the lactate threshold. Threshold-focused training blocks, while popular in many coaching traditions, appear to offer a poor return on investment in terms of adaptation relative to fatigue accumulation. Athletes transitioning from threshold-heavy programs to a polarized approach should expect an initial adjustment period and should progressively shift intensity distribution over several weeks rather than making an abrupt change.
