# What Is Best Practice for Training Intensity and Duration Distribution in Endurance Athletes?

## Metadata
- **Authors**: Stephen Seiler
- **Year**: 2010
- **Journal**: International Journal of Sports Physiology and Performance
- **DOI**: 10.1123/ijspp.5.3.276

## Abstract

The distribution of training intensity is among the most critical variables in endurance training prescription, yet the optimal approach has been a subject of ongoing debate within sports science. This review synthesizes evidence from descriptive studies of elite endurance athletes across multiple disciplines, including cross-country skiing, rowing, cycling, and distance running, to characterize common training intensity distribution patterns associated with world-class performance. Analysis of training logs, physiological testing data, and competition results reveals a remarkably consistent pattern among successful endurance athletes: approximately eighty percent of total training volume is performed at low intensity (below the first ventilatory or lactate threshold), with the remaining volume distributed between threshold work and high-intensity interval training above the second threshold. This pyramidal or polarized distribution contrasts sharply with the threshold-focused approach commonly adopted by recreational athletes and some coaching traditions, which emphasizes moderate-intensity training near the lactate threshold. Experimental evidence from controlled training studies comparing polarized, threshold, and high-volume low-intensity models supports the superiority of the polarized approach for maximizing endurance performance adaptations over training periods of six to sixteen weeks. The physiological rationale for polarized training centers on the principle that low-intensity training promotes peripheral adaptations including mitochondrial biogenesis and capillary density with minimal sympathetic stress, while high-intensity sessions provide potent stimuli for central cardiovascular adaptations and buffering capacity. Moderate-intensity threshold training, while producing acute metabolic stress, may generate insufficient stimulus for maximal adaptation at either end of the intensity spectrum while accumulating disproportionate autonomic and musculoskeletal fatigue. These findings have significant implications for how coaches and athletes structure training programs across mesocycles and competitive seasons.

## Key Findings

- Elite endurance athletes across multiple sports consistently train with approximately eighty percent of volume at low intensity, following a polarized distribution model
- Controlled studies show polarized training produces superior endurance adaptations compared to threshold-focused or exclusively high-volume approaches over six to sixteen week periods
- Moderate-intensity threshold training accumulates disproportionate fatigue relative to its adaptive stimulus compared to the combination of low and high intensity work
- Low-intensity training promotes peripheral adaptations (mitochondrial biogenesis, capillarization) while high-intensity work drives central cardiovascular improvements
- The pyramidal intensity distribution pattern is remarkably consistent across successful athletes in cycling, running, rowing, and cross-country skiing

## Practical Implications

Endurance athletes and coaches should structure training with a polarized intensity distribution, allocating roughly eighty percent of training time to low-intensity work below the first lactate threshold and reserving ten to twenty percent for high-intensity sessions above the second threshold. The common tendency among recreational athletes to train predominantly at moderate intensity should be actively discouraged, as this approach appears suboptimal for long-term performance development. Coaches should use objective intensity monitoring tools such as heart rate zones, power output, or pace targets to ensure athletes maintain truly low intensity during easy sessions rather than drifting into the moderate-intensity zone. High-intensity sessions should be strategically placed within the training week to allow adequate recovery between hard efforts.
