# Sleep in Elite Athletes and Nutritional Interventions to Enhance Sleep

## Metadata
- **Authors**: Shona L. Halson
- **Year**: 2014
- **Journal**: Sports Medicine
- **DOI**: 10.1007/s40279-014-0147-0

## Abstract

Sleep disturbance and inadequate sleep duration represent significant yet often underappreciated barriers to optimal athletic performance and recovery. This comprehensive review examines the prevalence of sleep disturbances in elite athletes, the physiological consequences of inadequate sleep for training adaptation and recovery, and evidence-based nutritional interventions that may enhance sleep quality and duration. Survey data from multiple sports reveal that elite athletes commonly report sleep durations below the recommended seven to nine hours, with particularly disrupted sleep patterns observed around competition, during travel across time zones, and in athletes with early morning training schedules. The physiological consequences of sleep deprivation in athletes include impaired glycogen resynthesis, elevated cortisol concentrations, reduced growth hormone secretion, compromised immune function, and impaired cognitive performance including decision-making and reaction time. Chronic sleep restriction may also alter appetite-regulating hormones, potentially affecting body composition management in weight-class athletes. Several nutritional strategies show promise for enhancing sleep in athletes. Tart cherry juice, which contains natural melatonin and anti-inflammatory compounds, has demonstrated improvements in sleep duration and quality in preliminary studies. High-glycemic-index carbohydrate meals consumed four hours before bedtime may reduce sleep onset latency by increasing tryptophan availability for serotonin and melatonin synthesis. Protein intake before sleep, particularly casein, may support overnight muscle protein synthesis while potentially improving sleep quality through tryptophan content. Micronutrient adequacy, particularly magnesium and zinc status, appears important for maintaining normal sleep architecture. This review highlights the need for individualized sleep optimization strategies that address the unique challenges faced by elite athletes and provides practical recommendations for nutritional approaches to support sleep quality.

## Key Findings

- Elite athletes commonly sleep fewer than seven hours per night, with sleep quality further compromised by competition anxiety, travel, and early training schedules
- Sleep deprivation impairs glycogen resynthesis, elevates cortisol, reduces growth hormone secretion, and compromises immune function in athletes
- Tart cherry juice containing natural melatonin improved sleep duration and quality in controlled trials with athletes
- High-glycemic-index carbohydrate consumption four hours before bedtime may reduce sleep onset latency through enhanced tryptophan availability
- Magnesium and zinc status are linked to sleep quality, and many athletes may have suboptimal levels of these micronutrients

## Practical Implications

Sports nutrition programs should incorporate sleep-supportive dietary strategies alongside traditional performance nutrition approaches. Athletes with sleep difficulties should consider tart cherry juice supplementation and timing of evening carbohydrate intake to support sleep onset. Magnesium and zinc status should be assessed and corrected if deficient. Coaches should structure training schedules to protect sleep opportunities, avoiding excessively early morning sessions when possible, and should recognize that sleep optimization may yield greater performance returns than many commonly used recovery modalities. Travel across time zones requires proactive sleep management strategies including light exposure timing and melatonin supplementation protocols tailored to the direction and magnitude of time zone changes.
