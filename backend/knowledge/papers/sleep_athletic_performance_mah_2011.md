# The Effects of Sleep Extension on the Athletic Performance of Collegiate Basketball Players

## Metadata
- **Authors**: Cheri D. Mah, Kenneth E. Mah, Eric J. Kezirian, William C. Dement
- **Year**: 2011
- **Journal**: Sleep
- **DOI**: 10.5665/SLEEP.1132

## Abstract

Sleep is increasingly recognized as a critical component of athletic recovery and performance, yet many competitive athletes report obtaining suboptimal sleep duration and quality during training and competition periods. This study investigated the effects of extended sleep on specific measures of athletic performance, reaction time, mood, and daytime sleepiness in collegiate basketball players. Eleven healthy male varsity basketball players maintained their habitual sleep patterns for a two-to-four-week baseline period, followed by a five-to-seven-week sleep extension period during which they aimed for a minimum of ten hours in bed each night. Performance testing was conducted regularly throughout both phases and included timed sprint drills, free throw shooting accuracy, three-point field goal accuracy, and reaction time assessments. Subjective mood and daytime sleepiness were evaluated using the Profile of Mood States and the Epworth Sleepiness Scale. Sleep extension resulted in significant improvements across all measured performance variables. Sprint times in a standardized court sprint decreased from an average of sixteen point two seconds to fifteen point five seconds. Free throw shooting accuracy improved by nine percent and three-point accuracy improved by nine point two percent. Reaction time on a psychomotor vigilance task improved significantly, and athletes reported enhanced subjective well-being with reduced fatigue and increased vigor scores. Daytime sleepiness scores decreased markedly, suggesting that athletes were chronically sleep-deprived during the baseline period despite self-reporting adequate sleep. These findings demonstrate that sleep extension provides measurable performance benefits across speed, accuracy, and cognitive function in competitive athletes, supporting the prioritization of sleep as a fundamental performance enhancement strategy.

## Key Findings

- Sleep extension to ten hours in bed per night improved sprint times by an average of 0.7 seconds in a standardized court sprint drill
- Shooting accuracy improved by approximately nine percent for both free throws and three-point field goals during the sleep extension period
- Reaction time improved significantly, indicating enhanced cognitive processing speed with extended sleep
- Athletes showed reduced daytime sleepiness scores during sleep extension, suggesting habitual sleep debt during normal training periods
- Mood profiles improved substantially, with decreased fatigue and increased vigor subscale scores

## Practical Implications

Athletes and coaches should prioritize sleep duration as a core component of training programs, targeting eight to ten hours of sleep per night during intensive training and competition periods. The significant performance improvements observed with sleep extension suggest that many athletes may be unknowingly operating below their performance potential due to chronic mild sleep restriction. Teams should consider implementing sleep education programs and structuring training and travel schedules to protect sleep opportunities. Sleep extension in the weeks leading up to important competitions may serve as a legal and accessible performance enhancement strategy. Monitoring subjective sleepiness and fatigue scores can help identify athletes who may benefit most from sleep interventions.
