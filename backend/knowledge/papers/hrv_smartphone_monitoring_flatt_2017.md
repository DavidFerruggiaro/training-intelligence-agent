# Evaluating Individual Training Adaptation With Smartphone-Derived Heart Rate Variability in Collegiate Female Soccer Players

## Metadata
- **Authors**: Andrew A. Flatt, Michael R. Esco
- **Year**: 2017
- **Journal**: Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research
- **DOI**: 10.1519/JSC.0000000000001095

## Abstract

The accessibility of heart rate variability (HRV) monitoring has been dramatically improved by the development of validated smartphone applications, yet research examining their practical application in team sport settings remains limited. This study evaluated the relationship between smartphone-derived HRV trends and individual training adaptation across a competitive soccer season in collegiate female athletes. Twenty-three NCAA Division I female soccer players performed daily morning HRV measurements using a validated smartphone application paired with a chest strap heart rate monitor throughout a sixteen-week competitive season. The ultra-short ln(RMSSD) protocol involved a sixty-second recording taken in the seated position immediately upon waking. Training load was quantified using session rating of perceived exertion (sRPE), while performance was assessed via repeated sprint ability tests conducted at pre-season, mid-season, and post-season time points. Athletes were retrospectively categorized as responders or non-responders based on performance changes. Analysis revealed that responders demonstrated progressively increasing or stable weekly HRV trends across the season, accompanied by lower weekly HRV coefficient of variation values. Non-responders exhibited declining HRV trends and greater weekly fluctuations, particularly during periods of fixture congestion and accumulated match exposure. Importantly, divergent HRV trends between responders and non-responders became apparent approximately four weeks before measurable performance differences emerged, suggesting a predictive capacity for HRV monitoring. The smartphone-based protocol demonstrated acceptable reliability and was well-tolerated by athletes, with compliance rates exceeding eighty-five percent across the season. These findings demonstrate that smartphone-derived HRV monitoring can serve as a practical early warning system for identifying athletes who may be failing to adapt positively to competitive training demands in collegiate team sport.

## Key Findings

- Smartphone-derived HRV monitoring successfully distinguished between responders and non-responders to training across a competitive soccer season
- Divergent HRV trends appeared approximately four weeks before measurable performance differences, demonstrating predictive value
- Lower weekly HRV coefficient of variation was associated with positive training adaptation, while elevated variability indicated maladaptation
- Ultra-short sixty-second seated recordings using smartphone applications provided acceptable reliability for longitudinal monitoring
- Athlete compliance with daily HRV measurement exceeded eighty-five percent, confirming practical feasibility in team sport settings

## Practical Implications

Collegiate and professional team sport programs can feasibly implement daily smartphone-based HRV monitoring to track individual athlete adaptation throughout competitive seasons. The seated sixty-second measurement protocol minimizes athlete burden and supports high compliance rates. Coaches should track both the weekly HRV trend (rolling average) and the coefficient of variation for each athlete individually to identify those who may be accumulating excessive fatigue or failing to adapt to training demands. Since HRV trend changes precede performance declines by several weeks, this approach enables proactive training load management, including targeted recovery interventions for athletes showing unfavorable HRV patterns before performance decrements become apparent.
