# Training Adaptation and Heart Rate Variability in Elite Endurance Athletes: Opening the Door to Effective Monitoring

## Metadata
- **Authors**: Daniel J. Plews, Paul B. Laursen, Andrew E. Kilding, Martin Buchheit
- **Year**: 2013
- **Journal**: Sports Medicine
- **DOI**: 10.1007/s40279-013-0071-8

## Abstract

Monitoring training adaptation in elite endurance athletes presents unique challenges, as traditional performance metrics may not capture the nuanced interplay between training load, recovery, and physiological adaptation. This review examines the current evidence for using heart rate variability (HRV) as a monitoring tool in elite endurance sport, with particular focus on the vagal-related index ln(RMSSD). The natural logarithm transformation of the root mean square of successive R-R interval differences provides a robust and practically accessible measure of cardiac parasympathetic modulation. Evidence from both cross-sectional and longitudinal studies demonstrates that elite endurance athletes typically exhibit elevated resting HRV compared to lesser-trained individuals, reflecting enhanced vagal tone as a hallmark of endurance training adaptation. However, the relationship between HRV and fitness is not linear at the highest levels; some highly trained athletes display paradoxical reductions in HRV indices, potentially reflecting autonomic saturation. Longitudinal monitoring reveals that progressive increases in training load are associated with initial HRV suppression followed by supercompensation during recovery phases, providing a physiological rationale for periodized training approaches. The coefficient of variation (CV) of daily HRV measurements has emerged as a particularly informative metric, with smaller day-to-day fluctuations associated with positive training adaptation and larger fluctuations potentially indicating maladaptation or non-functional overreaching. Practical recommendations include standardized morning measurements in the supine position, minimum recording durations of sixty seconds, and the use of weekly rolling averages to establish meaningful trends. This review provides a framework for integrating HRV monitoring into the daily training process of elite endurance athletes.

## Key Findings

- The ln(RMSSD) metric provides the most reliable and practically accessible vagal-related HRV index for athlete monitoring
- The coefficient of variation (CV) of daily HRV is a key indicator: lower CV suggests positive adaptation while elevated CV may signal maladaptation
- Elite athletes may show paradoxical HRV reductions despite high fitness due to autonomic saturation at extreme training loads
- HRV follows a suppression-supercompensation pattern during intensified training blocks followed by recovery periods
- Standardized morning supine measurements of at least 60 seconds duration provide the most reliable data for longitudinal tracking

## Practical Implications

Coaches and sport scientists working with endurance athletes should implement daily HRV monitoring using the ln(RMSSD) metric as part of a comprehensive athlete monitoring system. Both the absolute value and the day-to-day variability (CV) of HRV measurements provide complementary information about training status. A progressive decline in the rolling weekly HRV average accompanied by increasing CV may serve as an early warning sign of non-functional overreaching, prompting timely training load adjustments. Measurements should be taken upon waking in a standardized supine position for consistent results. This approach enables data-driven training decisions that can help prevent overtraining while maximizing the adaptive response to training.
