# Maximal Fat Oxidation During Exercise in Trained Men

## Metadata
- **Authors**: Juul Achten, Michael Gleeson, Asker E. Jeukendrup
- **Year**: 2002
- **Journal**: International Journal of Sports Medicine
- **DOI**: 10.1055/s-2002-32354

## Abstract

Fat oxidation during exercise varies substantially with exercise intensity, and identifying the intensity that maximizes fat utilization has important implications for both endurance performance and body composition management. This study determined the exercise intensity at which maximal fat oxidation (Fatmax) occurs in trained men and characterized the relationship between exercise intensity and fat oxidation rates across the full intensity spectrum. Eighteen well-trained male cyclists and triathletes performed graded exercise tests with continuous indirect calorimetry to measure substrate oxidation rates at each intensity stage. Exercise intensity was expressed as a percentage of maximal oxygen uptake (VO2max). Fat oxidation rates increased from rest through low-moderate intensities, reaching a peak at an average exercise intensity of sixty-four percent of VO2max, corresponding to seventy-four percent of maximal heart rate. Above this intensity, fat oxidation declined progressively with increasing reliance on carbohydrate metabolism, reaching minimal values at intensities exceeding eighty-five percent of VO2max. The zone of maximal fat oxidation (Fatmax zone) encompassed a range from fifty-five to seventy-two percent of VO2max, within which fat oxidation rates were within ten percent of the individual peak value. Considerable inter-individual variation was observed in both the absolute rate of maximal fat oxidation (range: zero point eighteen to zero point seventy-five grams per minute) and the intensity at which it occurred, underscoring the importance of individual assessment. Training status was a significant predictor of both absolute fat oxidation capacity and the intensity at which Fatmax occurred, with more highly trained individuals demonstrating higher peak fat oxidation rates at relatively higher exercise intensities. These findings establish that zone two training, encompassing the Fatmax zone, represents a specific and individually variable intensity range that can be precisely identified through metabolic testing and targeted in training prescription.

## Key Findings

- Maximal fat oxidation occurs at an average of sixty-four percent of VO2max (seventy-four percent of maximal heart rate) in trained men
- The Fatmax zone spans approximately fifty-five to seventy-two percent of VO2max, within which fat oxidation is near-maximal
- Substantial inter-individual variation exists in both the absolute fat oxidation rate and the intensity at which it peaks
- Training status positively correlates with higher absolute fat oxidation rates and a rightward shift of the Fatmax intensity
- Fat oxidation declines sharply above eighty-five percent of VO2max as carbohydrate metabolism becomes increasingly dominant

## Practical Implications

Endurance athletes seeking to enhance fat oxidation capacity should perform a significant proportion of training within the individually determined Fatmax zone, which broadly corresponds to zone two training intensity. Individual metabolic testing through graded exercise with gas exchange analysis or blood lactate profiling provides the most accurate method for identifying this zone for each athlete. In the absence of metabolic testing, training at sixty to seventy percent of VO2max or at an intensity described as comfortable and conversational provides a reasonable estimate for most trained individuals. Fat oxidation capacity is a trainable quality that improves with consistent zone two training, enabling athletes to spare glycogen at race intensities and delay fatigue onset during prolonged events. Body composition management strategies should also consider the Fatmax zone as a target intensity for maximizing fat utilization during exercise sessions.
