# The Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio Predicts Injury: High Chronic Workload May Decrease Injury Risk in Elite Rugby League Players

## Metadata
- **Authors**: Billy T. Hulin, Tim J. Gabbett, Peter Blanch, Phil Chapman, Dale Bailey, John A. Orchard
- **Year**: 2014
- **Journal**: British Journal of Sports Medicine
- **DOI**: 10.1136/bjsports-2013-092تین92

## Abstract

Monitoring training loads is essential for minimizing injury risk while promoting adaptation in elite athletes. This prospective cohort study investigated the relationship between the acute to chronic workload ratio and subsequent injury risk in fifty-three elite rugby league players over two competitive seasons. Training load was quantified using global positioning system technology to derive total distance, high-speed running distance, and acceleration and deceleration counts during all training sessions and matches. The acute workload was defined as the sum of the current week's training load, while the chronic workload was calculated as the rolling four-week average weekly training load. The acute to chronic workload ratio was computed for each player on a weekly basis. When acute to chronic workload ratios for total distance exceeded one point five, the likelihood of injury in the subsequent week was two to four times greater than when ratios were between zero point eight five and one point three five. Conversely, players with high chronic workloads who maintained acute to chronic ratios within the optimal range had significantly lower injury rates than players with low chronic workloads, even when exposed to similar acute loads. This protective effect of high chronic workload was evident across all external load metrics including total distance, high-speed running, and acceleration counts. The interaction between acute to chronic workload ratio and chronic workload fitness was significant, indicating that an athlete's training history moderates the relationship between workload spikes and injury occurrence. Players returning from injury who had low chronic workloads were particularly vulnerable to subsequent injury when exposed to rapid load increases. These findings demonstrate that the acute to chronic workload ratio provides a sensitive and specific tool for identifying athletes at elevated injury risk and that building robust chronic workloads through progressive training is protective against injury in elite rugby league.

## Key Findings

- Acute to chronic workload ratios exceeding one point five increased subsequent week injury likelihood by two to four times across all external load measures
- High chronic workload provided a protective effect against injury independent of the acute workload applied
- The interaction between ACWR and chronic workload was significant, confirming that training history moderates injury risk from load spikes
- Players returning from injury with low chronic workloads were most vulnerable to subsequent injury from rapid load increases
- High-speed running distance and acceleration metrics showed the strongest associations with subsequent injury risk

## Practical Implications

Performance staff should implement weekly ACWR monitoring for all players using GPS-derived external load metrics, with particular attention to high-speed running and acceleration demands. Load prescription should aim to maintain ACWR between zero point eight five and one point three five while progressively building chronic workload to develop physical resilience. Return-to-play protocols should include a structured load progression phase that rebuilds chronic workload before players are exposed to full match demands. Preseason programs should systematically develop chronic workload levels that prepare athletes for the acute demands of competition, as athletes entering the season with higher chronic workloads have greater protection against in-season injuries.
