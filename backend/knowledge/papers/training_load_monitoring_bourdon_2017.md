# Monitoring Athlete Training Loads: Consensus Statement

## Metadata
- **Authors**: Pitre C. Bourdon, Marco Cardinale, Andrew Murray, Paul Gastin, Michael Kellmann, Martin C. Varley, Tim J. Gabbett, Aaron J. Coutts, David B. Burgess, Wolfram Gregson, N. Timothy Cable
- **Year**: 2017
- **Journal**: International Journal of Sports Physiology and Performance
- **DOI**: 10.1123/ijspp.2017-0208

## Abstract

Effective training load monitoring is fundamental to the practice of evidence-based sport science and is essential for understanding the relationship between training, performance, and injury in athletes. This consensus statement, developed by an international panel of researchers and practitioners, provides a framework for implementing training load monitoring systems across sporting organizations. Training load is broadly categorized into external load, defined as the physical work performed by the athlete independent of internal characteristics, and internal load, defined as the relative physiological and psychological stress imposed on the athlete by the external load. External load measures include global positioning system derived metrics such as total distance, high-speed running distance, accelerations, and decelerations in field sports, as well as power output in cycling, swimming velocity, and barbell kinematics in strength training. Internal load measures include heart rate based methods such as training impulse (TRIMP) and summated heart rate zones, session rating of perceived exertion (session RPE), blood lactate concentrations, and subjective wellness indicators. The integration of both external and internal load monitoring provides the most comprehensive assessment of the training stimulus and athlete response. The acute to chronic workload ratio has emerged as a practical derivative metric that contextualizes current training load relative to the athlete's preparation history and has demonstrated utility for injury risk stratification across multiple sports. Effective monitoring systems require consistent data collection protocols, appropriate analytical methods including individualized thresholds and rolling averages, and timely communication of findings to coaching staff. Subjective measures including session RPE and daily wellness questionnaires provide valid and practical alternatives when technological monitoring is unavailable and correlate well with objective external and internal load measures. The consensus panel recommends that all sporting organizations implement systematic training load monitoring using a combination of objective and subjective measures appropriate to their sport, resources, and competitive context.

## Key Findings

- Training load monitoring should integrate both external load measures (work performed) and internal load measures (physiological and psychological response) for comprehensive assessment
- Session RPE is a valid and practical internal load measure that correlates strongly with heart rate based methods across multiple sports and exercise modalities
- The acute to chronic workload ratio provides effective injury risk stratification when applied with appropriate calculation methods and sport-specific thresholds
- Subjective wellness questionnaires capturing sleep quality, fatigue, muscle soreness, stress, and mood are sensitive indicators of training response and early overreaching
- Individualized monitoring thresholds based on each athlete's own data are more meaningful than population-based normative values

## Practical Implications

Sporting organizations should establish systematic training load monitoring as a foundational practice, beginning with session RPE and subjective wellness questionnaires as a minimum viable monitoring system. These low-cost subjective measures provide actionable data and can be supplemented with technology-based external load monitoring as resources allow. Data should be analyzed using rolling averages and acute to chronic workload ratios to contextualize daily loads within the athlete's training history. Communication between sport scientists and coaching staff should be frequent and focused on actionable insights rather than raw data, with clear decision rules for when training modifications are warranted. Monitoring systems should be sport-specific, reflecting the key physical demands and injury mechanisms relevant to each discipline.
