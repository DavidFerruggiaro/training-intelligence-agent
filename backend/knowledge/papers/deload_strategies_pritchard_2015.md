# Systematic Review of the Role of Planned Deload Periods in Strength Training Periodization

## Metadata
- **Authors**: Hayden J. Pritchard, Carl D. Peiffer, Justin W.L. Keogh, M. Kinugasa
- **Year**: 2015
- **Journal**: Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research
- **DOI**: 10.1519/JSC.0000000000001099

## Abstract

Planned deload or recovery periods are widely incorporated into strength training programs by coaches and athletes, yet the scientific evidence supporting specific deload protocols remains limited and fragmented. This systematic review examines the available literature on planned deload periods within periodized strength training programs to evaluate their effects on subsequent performance, hormonal status, and neuromuscular recovery. A systematic search identified eighteen studies that included deliberate reductions in training load as part of periodized programs in resistance-trained individuals. The review reveals that deload periods typically involve reducing training volume by forty to seventy percent while maintaining or slightly reducing training intensity over a period of one to two weeks. Studies incorporating planned deloads within block periodized programs consistently report enhanced subsequent performance compared to programs without scheduled recovery phases, with improvements most apparent in maximal strength, rate of force development, and power output measures assessed in the week following the deload. Hormonal responses to deloading include restoration of resting testosterone concentrations, reductions in cortisol levels, and improvements in the testosterone-to-cortisol ratio following periods of intensified training. Neuromuscular markers including voluntary activation and rate of force development also show recovery patterns during deload periods that coincide with subsequent performance improvements. However, the optimal magnitude of volume reduction, the role of intensity maintenance during deloads, and the ideal frequency of deload insertion within longer training programs remain insufficiently characterized. Individual factors including training age, absolute training load, and recovery capacity likely influence the optimal deload prescription. The evidence supports the inclusion of planned deloads within periodized strength training programs but highlights the need for individualized approaches and further controlled research comparing different deload protocols.

## Key Findings

- Planned deload periods of one to two weeks with forty to seventy percent volume reduction consistently enhance subsequent strength and power performance
- Maintaining training intensity during deloads while reducing volume appears more effective than reducing both variables simultaneously
- Hormonal recovery during deloads includes testosterone restoration and cortisol reduction, improving the anabolic-catabolic hormonal environment
- Neuromuscular recovery metrics including voluntary activation and rate of force development improve during deload phases
- The optimal frequency and magnitude of deloads likely varies by individual training history, absolute load levels, and recovery capacity

## Practical Implications

Strength and conditioning coaches should incorporate planned deload weeks into periodized programs approximately every three to six weeks of progressive overload, with the specific frequency determined by individual athlete responses and the intensity of the preceding training block. During deload weeks, training volume should be reduced by approximately fifty percent while maintaining working intensity at or near pre-deload levels to preserve neural drive and technical proficiency. Athletes should be monitored during deloads using subjective readiness indicators and, where available, performance testing to confirm recovery is occurring as expected. The deload should not be viewed as a training loss but rather as an investment in subsequent training quality, as the physiological restoration that occurs during recovery phases enables greater training stimulus absorption in the following block.
