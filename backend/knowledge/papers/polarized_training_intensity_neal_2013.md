# Six Weeks of a Polarized Training-Intensity Distribution Leads to Greater Physiological and Performance Adaptations Than a Threshold Model in Trained Cyclists

## Metadata
- **Authors**: Craig M. Neal, Angus M. Hunter, Lorraine Brennan, Aidan O'Sullivan, D. Lee Hamilton, Giuseppe De Vito, Stuart D.R. Galloway
- **Year**: 2013
- **Journal**: Journal of Applied Physiology
- **DOI**: 10.1152/japplphysiol.00652.2012

## Abstract

Endurance training prescription typically involves manipulating training intensity distribution across defined physiological zones, yet the comparative effectiveness of different intensity distribution models remains insufficiently studied in controlled experimental settings. This study compared the effects of six weeks of polarized versus threshold training intensity distribution on physiological adaptation and cycling performance in trained cyclists. Twelve well-trained male cyclists completed two six-week training interventions in a crossover design separated by a four-week washout period. During the polarized condition, cyclists performed approximately eighty percent of training time below the first lactate threshold and the remainder above the second lactate threshold, with minimal time spent between thresholds. During the threshold condition, cyclists performed approximately fifty-seven percent of training below the first threshold and forty-three percent between the first and second thresholds, with no training above the second threshold. Total training volume and energy expenditure were matched between conditions. Following the polarized training period, cyclists demonstrated significantly greater improvements in peak power output during incremental testing, lactate threshold power output, and forty-kilometer time trial performance compared to the threshold period. Muscle biopsy analysis revealed greater increases in mitochondrial enzyme activity (citrate synthase) and oxidative muscle fiber cross-sectional area following polarized training. Blood lactate concentrations at matched submaximal workloads were lower following polarized compared to threshold training, indicating enhanced metabolic efficiency. The crossover design strengthened these findings by controlling for individual variability in training response. These results demonstrate that a polarized intensity distribution elicits superior peripheral and central adaptations compared to a threshold model in trained cyclists, even when total training load is equated.

## Key Findings

- Polarized training produced significantly greater improvements in peak power output and forty-kilometer time trial performance versus threshold training over six weeks
- Muscle biopsy data showed greater increases in citrate synthase activity and oxidative fiber cross-sectional area with polarized training
- Submaximal blood lactate concentrations were lower after polarized versus threshold training at matched workloads, indicating improved metabolic efficiency
- The crossover design controlled for individual variation, strengthening the conclusion that intensity distribution rather than individual differences drove the results
- Total training volume and energy expenditure were matched between conditions, isolating the effect of intensity distribution

## Practical Implications

Trained cyclists seeking to maximize performance gains should structure their training with a clear polarized intensity distribution rather than spending extensive time at moderate threshold intensities. Even over a relatively short six-week mesocycle, the polarized approach yields measurably superior outcomes in both performance metrics and underlying physiological adaptations. Coaches should use power meter data to enforce strict zone boundaries, ensuring that easy rides remain below the first lactate threshold and that hard sessions include efforts above the second threshold. The temptation to gravitate toward moderate-intensity riding should be resisted, as this study demonstrates that matched training loads distributed in a polarized fashion produce better results at the cellular level.
