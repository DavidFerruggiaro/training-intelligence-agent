# Scientific Bases for Precompetition Tapering Strategies

## Metadata
- **Authors**: Inigo Mujika, Sabino Padilla
- **Year**: 2003
- **Journal**: Medicine and Science in Sports and Exercise
- **DOI**: 10.1249/01.MSS.0000084043.87404.3C

## Abstract

Tapering, the progressive reduction of training load prior to competition, represents a critical phase of athletic preparation that can significantly influence competitive performance outcomes. This review synthesizes the scientific evidence underlying tapering strategies, including the optimal manipulation of training variables during the taper period and the expected magnitude of performance improvement. Meta-analytic results from tapering studies across endurance and strength-power sports indicate that properly designed tapers can improve performance by an average of three percent, with individual improvements ranging from one to six percent depending on the sport, taper design, and preceding training phase characteristics. The primary training variable to reduce during a taper is training volume, which should be decreased by forty to sixty percent relative to pre-taper levels. Critically, training intensity should be maintained at pre-taper levels throughout the taper period, as reductions in intensity have been consistently associated with impaired performance outcomes and loss of training adaptations. Training frequency can be reduced modestly, though excessive frequency reduction may compromise neuromuscular coordination and sport-specific technical skills. The optimal taper duration is sport and individual-specific, ranging from approximately eight to fourteen days for most endurance sports. Progressive non-linear (exponential) tapers have demonstrated superior results compared to step or linear taper designs in controlled comparisons. The physiological mechanisms underlying taper-related performance improvements include recovery of neuromuscular function, restoration of hormonal balance (increased testosterone-to-cortisol ratio), replenishment of muscle glycogen stores, normalization of hematological parameters, resolution of exercise-induced muscle damage, and psychological freshness. Individual responses to tapering vary considerably, highlighting the importance of monitoring athlete readiness throughout the taper period and adjusting the taper parameters based on individual recovery trajectories rather than adhering rigidly to predetermined protocols.

## Key Findings

- Properly executed tapers improve competitive performance by an average of three percent, with individual gains of one to six percent
- Training volume should be reduced by forty to sixty percent during the taper while maintaining pre-taper training intensity
- Exponential (progressive non-linear) taper designs produce superior performance outcomes compared to step or linear tapers
- Optimal taper duration ranges from eight to fourteen days for most endurance sports, with individual variation requiring monitoring
- Reductions in training intensity during the taper are consistently associated with performance impairments and should be avoided

## Practical Implications

Coaches should plan a dedicated taper period of eight to fourteen days before major competitions, progressively reducing training volume by forty to sixty percent using an exponential decay pattern while maintaining or slightly increasing training intensity. Training frequency should be preserved near pre-taper levels to maintain neuromuscular coordination and sport-specific technical qualities. Athletes should be monitored throughout the taper using performance tests, subjective wellness questionnaires, and where available, hormonal and HRV markers to confirm adequate recovery is occurring. Individual taper responses should be documented across multiple competition cycles to refine each athlete's optimal taper parameters. Coaches should resist the temptation to reduce intensity during the taper, as maintaining high-quality sessions at reduced volume is the key principle for achieving peak performance on competition day.
