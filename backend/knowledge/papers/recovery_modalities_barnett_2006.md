# Using Recovery Modalities Between Training Sessions in Elite Athletes: Does It Help?

## Metadata
- **Authors**: Andrew Barnett
- **Year**: 2006
- **Journal**: Sports Medicine
- **DOI**: 10.2165/00007256-200636090-00005

## Abstract

Recovery from training and competition is becoming increasingly important to the high-performance athlete as the margin for gaining a competitive advantage through increased training volume and intensity decreases. Elite athletes frequently employ a range of recovery strategies including cold water immersion, contrast water therapy, compression garments, massage, active recovery, and stretching. Despite the widespread adoption of these modalities, their efficacy remains poorly substantiated by controlled research. This review examines the evidence for common recovery modalities used between training sessions in elite athletic populations. Cold water immersion at temperatures between ten and fifteen degrees Celsius for durations of ten to twenty minutes shows the most consistent evidence for reducing perceived muscle soreness and attenuating decrements in subsequent performance following intense exercise. The proposed mechanisms include hydrostatic pressure effects on fluid displacement, vasoconstriction reducing inflammatory cell infiltration, and analgesic effects on peripheral nerve conduction. Contrast water therapy alternating between warm and cold water shows moderate evidence for perceptual recovery benefits but limited evidence for physiological recovery enhancement compared to cold water immersion alone. Compression garments worn for extended periods following exercise demonstrate moderate evidence for reducing muscle soreness and maintaining subsequent power output, likely through reduced intramuscular space for swelling and improved venous return. Massage shows consistent benefits for perceived recovery and flexibility but equivocal evidence for performance restoration in controlled trials. Active recovery at low intensities facilitates lactate clearance but shows minimal benefits for subsequent performance beyond passive recovery when adequate time between sessions exists. The cumulative evidence suggests that no single recovery modality provides dramatic performance benefits, but the strategic combination of evidence-based approaches may provide meaningful cumulative advantages for athletes managing high training frequencies and competition demands.

## Key Findings

- Cold water immersion at ten to fifteen degrees Celsius for ten to twenty minutes shows the strongest evidence for reducing muscle soreness and preserving subsequent performance
- Contrast water therapy provides perceptual recovery benefits but limited physiological advantages over cold water immersion alone
- Compression garments worn post-exercise reduce muscle soreness and help maintain power output in subsequent sessions
- Massage consistently improves perceived recovery and flexibility but shows inconsistent effects on objective performance measures
- Active recovery accelerates lactate clearance but provides minimal performance benefits beyond passive rest when recovery time is adequate

## Practical Implications

Athletes and coaches should prioritize cold water immersion as the primary post-training recovery modality when managing high training loads or competing on consecutive days. Compression garments offer a practical passive recovery tool that can be worn during daily activities following training. Recovery strategies should be periodized alongside training, with more aggressive recovery protocols employed during high-load training blocks and competition periods. The psychological benefits of recovery rituals should not be underestimated, as perceived readiness strongly influences subsequent training quality. Coaches should develop individualized recovery protocols based on the athlete's training demands, response to specific modalities, and practical constraints of the training environment.
