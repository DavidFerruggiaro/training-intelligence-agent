# An Evidence-Based Approach for Choosing Post-Exercise Recovery Techniques to Reduce Markers of Muscle Damage, Soreness, Fatigue, and Inflammation

## Metadata
- **Authors**: Olivier Dupuy, Wafa Douzi, Dimitri Theurot, Laurent Bosquet, Benoit Dugué
- **Year**: 2018
- **Journal**: Frontiers in Physiology
- **DOI**: 10.3389/fphys.2018.00403

## Abstract

Post-exercise recovery techniques are widely used by athletes to accelerate the return to training readiness, yet evidence-based guidelines for selecting among available modalities remain lacking. This meta-analysis systematically evaluated the comparative effectiveness of popular recovery interventions on four key outcome domains: markers of muscle damage (creatine kinase), delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS), perceived fatigue, and inflammatory markers. A comprehensive search identified ninety-nine studies encompassing cold water immersion, contrast water therapy, whole-body cryotherapy, massage, compression garments, active recovery, stretching, electrical stimulation, and combined modality approaches. Network meta-analysis was employed to allow both direct and indirect comparisons between modalities across outcome domains. For reducing muscle damage markers, massage and cold water immersion demonstrated the largest effect sizes, with massage showing a standardized mean difference of negative zero point five four and cold water immersion negative zero point four two compared to passive recovery at twenty-four to forty-eight hours post-exercise. For attenuating DOMS, massage, compression garments, and cold water immersion were the most effective modalities, with significant reductions persisting up to ninety-six hours post-exercise. Perceived fatigue was most effectively reduced by massage and compression garments, while inflammatory markers responded most favorably to cold water immersion and whole-body cryotherapy. Stretching and active recovery showed minimal effects across all outcome domains when compared to passive recovery. The timing and duration of recovery interventions significantly moderated effect sizes, with earlier application post-exercise generally producing larger effects. Combined recovery approaches incorporating multiple modalities showed additive benefits in several studies. These findings provide a framework for evidence-based recovery prescription, enabling practitioners to select modalities targeting the specific recovery demands most relevant to their athlete's training context and competition schedule.

## Key Findings

- Massage produced the largest effect sizes for reducing creatine kinase levels and perceived muscle soreness across the recovery timeline
- Cold water immersion was most effective for reducing inflammatory markers and showed consistent benefits across all four outcome domains
- Compression garments demonstrated significant sustained effects on muscle soreness and perceived fatigue up to ninety-six hours post-exercise
- Stretching and active recovery showed minimal effects across all recovery outcome measures compared to passive rest
- Earlier application of recovery modalities post-exercise generally produced larger beneficial effects across all outcome domains

## Practical Implications

Recovery protocols should be matched to the primary recovery demand facing the athlete. When muscle damage is the primary concern following eccentric-heavy or unaccustomed training, massage and cold water immersion should be prioritized. For managing perceived fatigue during congested competition schedules, compression garments offer a practical continuous recovery tool. Cold water immersion remains the most versatile single modality with benefits across all recovery domains. Coaches should consider implementing multi-modal recovery protocols that combine complementary approaches to address multiple recovery dimensions simultaneously. Recovery modalities should be applied as soon as practically possible following training to maximize their effectiveness.
