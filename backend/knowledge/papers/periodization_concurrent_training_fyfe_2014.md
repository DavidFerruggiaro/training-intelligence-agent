# The Role of Training Intensity in Concurrent Aerobic and Strength Training: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

## Metadata
- **Authors**: Jackson J. Fyfe, David J. Bishop, Nigel K. Stepto
- **Year**: 2014
- **Journal**: Sports Medicine
- **DOI**: 10.1007/s40279-014-0190-x

## Abstract

Concurrent training, the simultaneous integration of resistance and endurance exercise within a periodized program, is essential for sports requiring both strength and aerobic capacity yet remains complicated by the well-documented interference effect. This systematic review and meta-analysis examines the influence of training intensity on the magnitude of the interference effect during concurrent training programs. A comprehensive literature search identified thirty-one studies meeting inclusion criteria, encompassing over five hundred participants performing concurrent resistance and endurance training compared with single-mode resistance training controls. Meta-analytic results confirmed that concurrent training attenuates maximal strength and hypertrophy gains compared with resistance training alone, with the magnitude of interference modulated by the intensity and modality of the endurance training component. High-intensity endurance training, particularly running, produced greater interference with lower body strength and power development compared to moderate-intensity cycling-based endurance training. The molecular mechanisms underlying the interference effect center on the competing signaling pathways activated by endurance and resistance exercise. Endurance exercise activates adenosine monophosphate-activated protein kinase (AMPK) and peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma coactivator one-alpha (PGC-1alpha), promoting mitochondrial biogenesis and oxidative metabolism. Resistance exercise activates the mechanistic target of rapamycin (mTOR) pathway, driving muscle protein synthesis and hypertrophy. Cross-talk between these pathways, particularly AMPK-mediated inhibition of mTOR signaling, provides a molecular basis for the interference effect. Temporal separation of training sessions by at least six to eight hours and strategic sequencing of session order within training days may partially mitigate interference. Lower-intensity and non-weight-bearing endurance modalities produced less interference with strength development, suggesting that training intensity prescription is a critical variable in concurrent program design. These findings provide evidence-based guidelines for minimizing the interference effect while achieving concurrent development of strength and endurance qualities.

## Key Findings

- Concurrent training significantly attenuates maximal strength and hypertrophy gains compared to resistance training alone, confirming the interference effect
- High-intensity running produces the greatest interference with lower body strength development compared to cycling or other non-weight-bearing modalities
- The molecular interference mechanism involves AMPK activation from endurance exercise inhibiting mTOR-mediated protein synthesis stimulated by resistance exercise
- Temporal separation of resistance and endurance sessions by six to eight hours partially mitigates the interference effect at the molecular signaling level
- Lower-intensity endurance training produces less interference with strength adaptation than high-intensity endurance work

## Practical Implications

Athletes and coaches designing concurrent training programs should consider several strategies to minimize the interference effect. Endurance sessions should be separated from resistance sessions by at least six hours when possible, with resistance training prioritized earlier in the day if strength development is the primary goal. Non-weight-bearing endurance modalities such as cycling may produce less interference with lower body strength than running-based training. When concurrent sessions must be performed on the same day, performing resistance training first may preserve the acute mTOR signaling response needed for strength adaptation. High-intensity interval endurance work should be used judiciously during periods when maximal strength development is prioritized. Block periodization approaches that emphasize either strength or endurance within focused mesocycles may offer a practical alternative to daily concurrent training.
